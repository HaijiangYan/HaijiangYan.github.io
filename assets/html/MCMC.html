MCMC: Monte Carlo, Markov Chain and sampling

24/July/2023, Haijiang

• What is Monte carlo?

As name of a casino, Monte Carlo methods denotes a kind of stochastic simulation algorithms which are used to approximate numerical results by repeated random sampling, e.g., tricky or even computationally untractable integral in early stage like:

$$ \begin{equation} \int_a^bf(x)dx \end{equation} $$

The integral could be untractable if `f(x)` cannot be antiderivatived, so that we need another way to work around. A wise candidate is to draw samples uniformly `{x_1, x_2, ...x_n}` within `[a, b]` to produce an estimate of `f_{mean}(x)=frac{sum_{i=0}^nf(x_i)}{n}` (the average height), and then multiplied by the width `(b-a)` to generate the approximated integral. As such,

$$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} \int_a^bf(x)dx&\approx(b-a)\frac{\sum_{i=1}^nf(x_i)}{n}, x_i\sim U(a, b) \\\\ &=E_{x\sim U(a, b)}(b-a)f(x) \end{split} \end{equation} $$

The solution above is simple and elegant, producing exact outcome as long as we have enough samples. However, the underlying assumption that `x` is uniformly distributed is just a specification. Actually `x` could be any distribution over `[a, b]`, so we must move on to take the distribution `p(x)` into consideration.

$$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} \int_a^bf(x)dx&=\int_a^b\frac{f(x)}{p(x)}p(x)dx \\\\ &\approx\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{f(x_i)}{p(x_i)}}{n}, x_i\sim p(x) \\\\ &=E_{p(x)}(\frac{f(x)}{p(x)}) \end{split} \end{equation} $$

So you can see that uniform distribution is just a specification when `p(x)=frac{1}{b-a}`. With such an approximation method, we can turn whatever tricky integral (or any other computation theoretically) into a question like "how to effectively draw samples from a given distribution `p(x)`?".
